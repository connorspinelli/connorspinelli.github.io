01 Junior Design

Wrist Support Structure for Catheter Insertion

Designed to maintain proper wrist positioning and radial artery catheter securement during medical procedures, this orthotic support aims to reduce complications from catheter dislodgement and ensure consistent pressure readings. Taking the lead on concept generation, prototyping, and engineering design, the project progressed through multiple iterations before converging on a final design CNC machined from Delrin. A custom lathed hinge pin maintains the axis of rotation for the plunger spring pin mechanism, enabling repeatable, reliable positioning across a range of clinical settings.

SolidWorks 3D Printing Prototypes Design for Manufacturability
02 Research Analysis

Effects of Pre-Processing Parameters on Stretch Reflex EMG: A Comparative Analysis

There is no universal standard for surface EMG signal processing in stretch reflex studies, leaving open the question of whether conclusions about long-latency reflex amplitude depend on the chosen filtering pipeline. To address this, eight distinct pre-processing pipelines were applied to recorded subject data and their statistical effects on LLR amplitude systematically compared. The analysis confirmed that our bio signal analysis methodology is reliable; study conclusions are robust and independent of variations in filtering technique, validating the pipeline used across our lab's ongoing research.

03 Research Engineering

Robot Support Structure for Stroke Patients Participating in MRI StretchWrist Studies

Designed an MRI-compatible structural mounting system that positions and supports stroke patients' arms inside a high-field MRI bore during robotic wrist perturbation studies. All materials were carefully selected for MRI safety - no ferromagnetic components - while maintaining sufficient stiffness to prevent movement artifacts. The structure integrates with the existing StretchWrist robot and accommodates a range of patient arm sizes and stroke-related mobility constraints.

SolidWorks Woodworking MRI Safety
04 Research Engineering

Robot Control Box for MRI StretchWrist Robot

Engineered the control electronics enclosure for the MRI StretchWrist robotic therapy system, ensuring reliable signal transmission and cable management in a high-field MRI environment. The enclosure organizes control cables, pneumatic lines, and sensor leads while providing RF shielding to prevent image artifacts. Designed for fast setup during experimental sessions and straightforward maintenance by research staff.

SolidWorks Laser Cutting Soldering RF Shielding
05 Mechanical Design Course

Tolerancing Project for Mechanical Design Course

Applied GD&T principles and statistical tolerancing analysis to a multi-component mechanical assembly, balancing precision requirements against manufacturing cost and feasibility. Worst-case and root-sum-square (RSS) tolerance stack-up analyses were performed to verify that all mating interfaces met fit and function specifications across the full manufacturing variation range. The project produced complete engineering drawings with dimensioned, toleranced geometry per ASME Y14.5 standards.

06 Computational Modeling

Modeling the Concentration of Digoxin Activity after Intravenous Injection in Three Compartments

Developed a three-compartment pharmacokinetic model to simulate the distribution, redistribution, and elimination of digoxin following intravenous injection, capturing drug behavior across plasma, peripheral tissues, and a deep tissue compartment. Research was supported by AI workflows, using NotebookLM for literature synthesis and Claude to accelerate MATLAB simulation development, practicing prompt engineering for efficiency, reliability, and scalability. Results clearly illustrate digoxin's narrow therapeutic window and the critical importance of precise dosing in cardiac care.

07 Clinical Biomechanics Research

The Impact of Graduated Backpack Loading on Sagittal Plane Kinematics and Joint Kinetics During Gait

This study examined how progressively increasing backpack loads (0%, 10%, and 20% of body weight) affect sagittal-plane walking mechanics in healthy young adults. Fifteen participants completed overground walking trials while trunk lean, hip angle, and knee angle were tracked using 2D motion capture in Kinovea. Results showed consistent numerical trends across all conditions: trunk forward lean increased from 7.96 degrees at baseline to 12.07 degrees at 20% body weight, hip flexion increased, and knee posture became more extended under heavier loads. Statistical analysis via repeated-measures ANOVA in JMP revealed no significant group-level effect, reflecting the high individual variability in how healthy young adults compensate for added load. The findings suggest that loads approaching 20% body weight produce observable but highly individualized gait adaptations, and that universal safe-load thresholds may not apply across all individuals.
